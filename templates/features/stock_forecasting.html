Cycle-Based Price Forecasting

Our stock forecasting feature uses detected market cycles to project potential future price movements. By understanding the length, strength, and current phase of dominant cycles, we can create forecasts that help visualize how these cycles might influence price in the coming days and weeks.

Important Note

These forecasts are projections based on mathematical analysis of historical cycles and should not be interpreted as guaranteed predictions. Markets are influenced by many factors beyond cyclical patterns, including news events, economic reports, and unexpected developments that no model can predict with certainty.

How Our Forecasting Works

  1. Cycle Identification

    We first identify dominant cycles in historical price data using Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) analysis.

  2. Cycle Parameterization

    For each dominant cycle, we determine its period (length), amplitude (strength), and phase (current position).

  3. Cycle Projection

    Using these parameters, we extend each cycle forward in time to project its future path.

  4. Cycle Superposition

    We combine multiple cycle projections, giving more weight to stronger cycles, to create a composite forecast.

  5. Visualization

    The final forecast is presented as an interactive chart showing both historical data and projected price movement.

Cycle Superposition Principle

Our forecasting model is based on the principle of cycle superposition, which states that complex price movements can be broken down into simpler cyclic components, and these components can be recombined to project future movement.

Mathematical Foundation

For each detected cycle, we model its contribution as:

C(t) = A × cos(2π × t/P + φ)

Where A is amplitude, P is period, φ is phase, and t is time. The total forecast is the sum of all cycle contributions plus the last known price.

Weighted Influence

Not all cycles are created equal. In our model:

  • Stronger cycles (higher amplitude) have more influence on the forecast
  • More recent cycles are given higher weight than older patterns
  • Shorter cycles may diminish in importance over longer forecast horizons

Forecast Interpretation Guidelines

Forecast Horizon

Forecasts are typically most reliable in the near term (1-2 weeks) and become less certain as the horizon extends.

Direction vs. Magnitude

Focus more on the projected direction of price movement rather than exact price targets.

Regular Updates

Forecasts should be updated regularly as new data becomes available.

Common Forecast Patterns

Pattern Visual Characteristics Interpretation
Upward Trajectory Forecast line trending upward Multiple cycles aligned in bullish phase
Downward Trajectory Forecast line trending downward Multiple cycles aligned in bearish phase
Consolidation Relatively flat or choppy forecast Cycles in transitional phases or conflicting
Reversal Point Forecast shows inflection from up to down (or vice versa) Potential turning point as cycles reach extremes
Wave Pattern Clear up and down oscillations Strong single dominant cycle influencing price
Practical Application

The most effective way to use our forecasts is as part of a comprehensive trading approach. Consider them alongside traditional technical analysis, fundamental analysis, market sentiment, and risk management strategies. Pay particular attention to forecast turning points that align with other technical indicators.
